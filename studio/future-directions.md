AI holds tremendous potential for humanities research, but it's not the whole picture. Amaranth's developing research program runs from AI-assisted workflows to 3D cultural heritage documentation to tactile pedagogy and community engagement. The approach is the same across all of it: experiment carefully, document profusely, and share what we learn.
{: .lead}

AI is doing something to the humanities that feels simultaneously exciting and unsettling. The tools that can now search across thousands of archival documents, surface patterns across entire genres, and make oral history collections navigable in an afternoon are the same tools that fabricate sources, flatten nuance, and reflect the biases of whatever they were trained on. 

The skepticism is warranted. So is the curiosity. Humanists—who have spent careers asking whose knowledge counts, how authority gets constructed, and what gets left out of any account—are exactly the people who should be working through these questions rather than waiting while others decide what AI is for.


## The methodological gap
The humanities have produced a lot of good thinking about AI. There's a growing body of work theorizing AI as a cultural object—what it means for authorship, interpretation, and the construction of knowledge. There's highly technical work on benchmarks aimed at computational specialists. And there are serious conversations about the ethical and methodological challenges AI poses for humanistic materials: questions of sensitivity, provenance, and control that don't disappear just because the technology advances. What's been harder to find is the practical layer between the theory and the benchmarks. That's where Amaranth's work lives.


## Research tracks
Our developing research program runs along a few tracks that connect to each other.

- We're building and documenting AI-assisted research workflows—the practical methods for using AI carefully in humanities research that the field is talking about but hasn't produced in usable, reproducible form. 
- We're investing in digital cultural heritage work: 3D scanning, modeling, and processing as tools for deep humanities research in a region whose heritage demands that kind of attention.
- We're developing tactile pedagogy and community engagement through 3D printing—making research-grounded objects that can travel into classrooms, K-12 programs, and community spaces. Throughout, we continue the class integration and public scholarship work that has been central to Amaranth from the start.



## Why local, why here

Researcher-controlled AI—models running on hardware you control rather than sending materials to a remote service—opens possibilities that aren't available otherwise. Materials stay closer to their context. Provenance is preserved. The research process is more transparent: you can see what the model is doing with your materials, adjust it, and document it in ways that make the work reproducible and citable.

Some materials also require particular care. New Mexico's cultural heritage—its Indigenous collections, community oral histories, archival materials held by families and organizations—carries ethical responsibilities that shape how AI can responsibly be applied. Communities whose materials these are have every reason to ask how AI was applied to them—and researcher-controlled workflows make that question answerable in ways that a terms-of-service agreement simply can't.

University AI infrastructure is developing quickly and will address many of these questions at scale. But the habits of careful, provenance-aware AI practice are worth developing now—and they'll transfer well to whatever infrastructure comes next. New Mexico's rich and varied cultural landscape makes UNM a natural place to develop them.


## Humanities + AI research workflows

The core of Amaranth's AI research work is developing and documenting workflows for corpus-scale analysis with verifiable provenance. Not asking AI for conclusions—using locally controlled models to help compare texts, images, and 3D-derived views at a scale that would otherwise be impossible to hold in view, while keeping every AI-generated interpretation traceable back to the underlying sources that produced it.

We're building this on real materials: hundreds of medical texts from the 18th through 20th centuries, manuscripts from 14th-century Spain, ongoing work with the Maxwell Museum's collections, and projects emerging from community partnerships like oral histories of Rio Grande farmers. Real corpora, real questions, and enough source familiarity to ground-truth AI output.

This work also primes the pump for the institutionally gated AI infrastructure that's coming to universities. When researchers have experience directing AI workflows, evaluating outputs critically, and understanding where the models go wrong, they're far better positioned to use those institutional tools well—and to help shape how they're implemented.


## Digital cultural heritage workflows
New Mexico's cultural heritage is extraordinarily rich and extraordinarily varied—and much of it has never been systematically documented. We're developing workflows for 3D scanning, modeling, and processing collections at scale: Indigenous ceramics, colonial-era manuscripts, archaeological sites, objects held by families and organizations who don't have the resources for formal digitization. The goal is research-grade documentation that captures the information humanists actually need—texture, dimension, material, spatial relationship—not just an image.

This work connects directly to the AI research workflows track. Scan-derived imagery becomes a parallel input alongside text, enabling the kind of visual analysis at scale that close looking alone can't reach. We're building this with real collections: ongoing work with the Maxwell Museum, the Objex archive, and projects emerging from community partnerships. Better scanning produces richer AI-assisted analysis. Better analysis produces documentation worth sharing.

## Tactile pedagogy and community engagement

Digital scholarship should reach people beyond the people who produced it. 3D printing is one of the most direct ways to make that happen—and this track depends on the scanning and modeling work in the digital cultural heritage pipeline.

A clay print of a ceremonial vessel, held in a student's hands, does something a photograph on a screen cannot. Terrain maps let you trace a landscape with your fingers. Architectural replicas make comparative scale tangible in a way no image can. These aren't novelties—they're pedagogical and community engagement infrastructure. They're also a deliberate escape from what we've started calling "trinketland": the tendency to treat 3D printing as keychain production rather than research-grounded material reconstruction that invites humanistic inquiry.

The prints that result can travel—into classrooms, into K-12 programs, into community spaces, into conversations about heritage, access, and repatriation that will remain important for decades as this technology evolves. When a community can hold a replica of an object they've never been able to see up close, that changes what conversations are possible.

## Workflows as scholarship

The workflows themselves are a research output. Not just the articles and projects that result from using them, but the documented, version-controlled, citable records of how the work was done—what was tried, what the model produced, what worked, what didn't, and how the process could be reproduced or adapted.

Amaranth is building toward a public site where AI research workflows live openly. This isn't supplementary documentation tacked on after the fact. It's the practical layer the field is missing, and making it open is part of the contribution. When another humanist at another institution can read exactly how we approached a corpus, what parameters we used, where the model surprised us and where it failed us, that's scholarship doing what scholarship is supposed to do.

Xanthan is the publication infrastructure that makes this sustainable: open-source, freely hosted, designed so that other researchers can build on the same foundation without starting from scratch.


## Building hopefully

We're not claiming to have any of this figured out.

But the infrastructure is coming together, and the questions are urgent. The direction feels clear enough: open workflows, documented and shared; students and faculty who can direct AI-assisted research rather than just use it; communities whose histories and materials are partners in the work rather than data to be processed; the humanities at the center of how this institution navigates what AI is for. We want students at every level to have a chance to be part of this—including those who might not encounter it through coursework.

That's not a modest ambition. But we're in a good place to pursue it, and we'd rather do it in good company. If any of this resonates—whether you have a research question, a community connection, or just a hunch—come find us.
