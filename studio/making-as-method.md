## Every design choice is an argument

Building is a form of thinking. When you make a website for your research, a poster for a conference, an oral history project, a podcast, or a 3D-printed object for a class, you face decisions that prose alone never forces: Who is this for? What does it feel like to encounter? What goes first? What evidence needs context? What should a reader, listener, viewer, or participant be able to do next?

Those decisions are not peripheral to scholarship. They shape what an audience can understand, what they notice, what they trust, and whether they keep going.
{: .lead}

Every design choice is an argument. The structure of a website tells readers what you think matters. A typeface says something about the register of the work. An image is an editorial claim. A map makes choices about scale, movement, and absence. A 3D print asks what material encounter can teach that a flat image cannot.

Craft is not decoration applied after the thinking is done. It is part of the thinking. And when you share something you have built, you discover what you actually think. Vague ideas do not survive contact with a real audience.


## Form changes the work

Different forms ask different questions.

A website asks how a reader moves through evidence. A poster asks what can be understood at a glance. A podcast asks how an argument sounds when it has to hold attention through voice, pacing, and story. An oral history asks what ethical responsibilities come with listening, editing, describing, and preserving another person's account. A 3D object asks what scale, texture, and handling can reveal about material culture.

These are not just delivery formats. They are scholarly situations. They force choices about audience, emphasis, sequence, access, credit, and care.


## Digital literacy through real work

Amaranth's teaching work is built around capability, not dependency. Students learn more when they make something real: a public essay, a collaborative archive, a recorded interview, a tactile teaching object, a project site that has to make sense to someone beyond the instructor.

That work builds digital literacy in a practical and humanistic sense. Students learn how platforms shape knowledge, how metadata gives structure to materials, how images and layout guide interpretation, how AI can help with technical tasks, and why human judgment still has to direct the process.

The point is not to make every student a designer, developer, audio engineer, or fabrication specialist. The point is to help them understand enough to make choices, evaluate results, ask better questions, and take responsibility for what they publish.


## AI changes the technical threshold, not the intellectual work

AI can make technical beginnings less punishing. It can help draft code, structure messy data, summarize transcripts, generate alternate layouts, or explain an unfamiliar workflow. That matters. It means students and scholars can experiment earlier, with less time lost to blank screens and syntax errors.

But AI does not make judgment optional. Someone still has to know what the project is trying to say, what evidence supports it, what has been distorted, what needs verification, and what should be left out. Humanistic habits of close reading, source criticism, historical context, and attention to power are not obstacles to digital work. They are what make digital work worth doing.
