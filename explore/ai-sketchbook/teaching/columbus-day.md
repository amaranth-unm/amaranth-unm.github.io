Ask AI to perform a historical analysis of a short article from 1892 announcing President Harrison's establishment of Columbus Day as a federal holiday. Students will see different analysis generated by chatbots when logged into their personal account versus the content generated on incognito mode. 

A filter bubble is the term for when a user’s experience with digital content is different from others based on information gathered about them. When encountering this type of behavior in search results, there could be one of three things happening. The user might have input customizations for their account, as ChatGPT allows for that in its settings. Another filtering mechanism is the guardrails put into the system, designed to avoid scenarios in which the chatbot might encourage someone to do harm to themselves or others. This is carried out by human labor to train the social harm out of a newly created AI chatbot. For example, ChatGPT is trained by hundreds of low-paid workers in Kenya to rate certain answers as off-limits. The third type of filter is algorithmic, meaning that the output is different based on factors that the system perceives about the user during the interaction. For example, if you search Google for best hospitals, you get different results if you are doing that search in Albuquerque or London because Google uses your location data to adjust the results it gives you. For the Columbus Day document, ChatGPT made my answer more complex based on its experience with my logged-in account.

## How It Works
This works best with students working individually so that they can compare the diversity in responses using their own chatbot accounts as well as the variety that might emerge from incognito chats. 

This lesson is based on Chapter 4 of Sam Wineburg's book *Why Learn History When It's Already on Your Phone.* Wineburg relates how he gave an exercise to Jacob, a successful, high achieving AP US History student at a Bay Area high school, and then gave the same exercise to a group of history graduate students specializing in fields other than US History. He provides them only with a copy of the short *New York Times* article from July 22, 1892, about Columbus Day. He tells them simply to provide a historical analysis of the document. Jacob talked cogently about what Columbus did and whether it’s justified to honor him with a federal holiday. The graduate students, instead, immediately started to wonder about the context of late nineteenth century US politics, bringing up demographic trends and the rise of nativism. The graduate students spent no time evaluating Columbus’s actions and instead kept their analysis on the politcal pressures that influenced the decision-making process of President Benjamin Harrison. Jacob issued pronouncements, reaching a conclusion about Columbus, and the graduate students raised questions, puzzling about late nineteenth century politics and society. 

In response to my prompt to provide a historical analysis of the Columbus Day proclamation, ChatGPT provided a three-page analysis that included sentences about the 1890s as 'marked by intense debates over immigration and national identity. Southern and Eastern European immigrants, Catholic and Jewish communities, and labor movements were transforming the urban landscape.' No puzzlement, but it did sound to me like high quality historical thinking on par with the contextual focus that characterized the graduate students’ responses. But I was inside my own filter bubble.

When responding to an anonymous user without an account, ChatGPT did address the historical context of immigration in the late nineteenth century rather than only evaluating Columbus himself. However, unlike the answer provided to my account, ChatGPT included two paragraphs on the moral dimension of honoring Columbus. It also left out Catholics and lacked any critique of assimilation as a cultural script.

Now, therefore, I, Benjamin Harrison, President of the United States of America, in pursuance of the aforesaid joint resolution do hereby appoint Friday, Oct. 21, 1892, the four hundredth anniversary of the discovery of America by Columbus, as a general holiday for the people of the United States. On that day let the people so far as possible cease from toil and devote themselves to such exercises as may best express honor to the discoverer and their appreciation of the great achievements of the four completed centuries of American life. Columbus stood in his age as the pioneer of progress and enlightenment. The system of universal education is in our age the most prominent and salutary feature of the spirit of enlightenment, and it is peculiarly appropriate that the schools be made by the people the centre of the day's demonstration. Let the national flag float over every school house in the country, and the exercises be such as shall impress upon our youth the patriotic duties of American citizenship. In the churches and in the other places of assembly of the people, let there be expressions of gratitude to Divine Providence for the devout faith of the discoverer, and for the Divine care and guidance which has directed our history and so abundantly blessed our people. In testimony whereof, I have hereunto set my hand and caused the seal of the United States to be affixed. Done at the City of Washington, this 21st day of July, in the year of our Lord one thousand eight hundred and ninety-two and of the independence of the United States the one hundred and seventeenth. BENJAMIN HARRISON.

## Why This One Lands

This exercise gives students real-world experience with the operation of filter bubbles while at the same time ingraining the cognitive strategies of historical thinking. The students see that when some historical context goes missing, the meaning of the narrative changes dramatically. 

## Caveats

As the LLM training set starts to include more about historical thinking, the chatbots might be less likely to leave out relevant context when asked to perform a 'historical analysis.'
